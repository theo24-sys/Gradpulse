The Challenge

A Structural Transformation in the World of Work

The World Economic Forum projects that by 2025, technological advancements will require 50% of the global workforce to reskill — while 85 million jobs are redefined or displaced, yet 97 million new roles emerge for those equipped with the right capabilities.

The Skill Mismatch

In Africa, graduates exit institutions carrying theoretical credentials but not the market-ready competencies employers demand. The result: qualified on paper, unadaptable in practice.

Upskilling

The enhancement of existing competencies to meet evolving role demands. A strategic imperative that GradPulse industrialises at the point of learning, not post-graduation.

Reskilling

The acquisition of entirely new skills to transition into new functions. GradPulse makes this accessible, affordable, and structural — not episodic — for every student.

Vision

To ensure that the educational curriculum meets market standards and needs, by providing practical simulations on real market demands.

Mission

To enable a 100% transition rate from classroom to marketplace.

Change Theory

If students develop market-ready skills, then they will find or create jobs that industrialise Africa affordably.

Quality We hold our skill outputs to market standards, not institutional averages.
Reliability Students, universities, and corporations depend on us for consistent, evidence-based outcomes.
Innovation We apply the latest in scenario-based learning design and adaptive technology.

Component 1

GradPulse UniSmart

High School to University Transition Platform

Every year, thousands of high school students make university and programme choices based on prestige, parental influence, or peer pressure — with little visibility into where the labour market is heading. UniSmart resolves this by placing a market-facing lens on the most consequential academic decision a young person will make.

Grounded in Human Capital Theory (Becker & Schultz, 1962), UniSmart transforms university selection from a guesswork process into an evidence-led decision.

Target Users: High school students (Grades 10–12), parents, school career counsellors, and high school institutions.

Component 2 — Core Platform

GradPulse Campus

University to Marketplace — Scenario-Based Learning Model

A university degree is no longer sufficient proof of workforce readiness. GradPulse Campus is a skill-first, scenario-based learning environment designed for university students that directly intersects the academic curriculum with real-time market problems submitted by corporations.

Students do not study about industry challenges in textbooks — they solve actual ones, submitted by actual companies, in real time. The platform operationalises Structural Learning Theory (Scandura, 1973) and Expectancy Theory (Vroom, 1964).

Target Users: Undergraduate and postgraduate students, university faculties, and graduates seeking to close skill gaps.
Join Campus

Curriculum Integration Module

Practitioner Feedback Loop

Component 3

GradPulse Corporate

Corporate Partnership & Talent Customisation Platform

The cost of upskilling and reskilling in the corporate sector is significant and growing. GradPulse Corporate gives organisations a direct, structured channel into the student talent pool — allowing them to submit real business challenges, engage with verified problem-solvers, customise their future workforce, and reduce internal upskilling costs.

Rather than hiring graduates and then investing in their development, organisations invest in development before hiring. Grounded in Sociotechnical Systems Theory (Eric & Ken, 1950).

Target Users: Corporates, early-stage companies, lean teams, individual project owners, and industry associations.

Evidence-Based

Built on Research, Not Assumptions

GradPulse's approach is grounded in peer-reviewed research and established theoretical frameworks, ensuring every feature delivers measurable impact.

Structural Learning Theory

Scandura (1973) — Learners who engage with real underlying problems build transferable, adaptive competencies.

Human Capital Theory

Becker & Schultz (1962) — Investment in skills at the right stage produces compounding returns in productivity and employability.

Expectancy Theory

Vroom (1964) — Students see a direct link between effort, solvability points, and ranking, creating tangible rewards.

Sociotechnical Systems

Eric & Ken (1950) — Optimal performance requires joint optimisation of people and technology.
